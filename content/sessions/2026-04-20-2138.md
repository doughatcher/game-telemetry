The vessel cut through the last familiar waters before evening, and then the sea changed. It was not a dramatic thing — no wall of fog, no sudden drop in temperature — but every member of the party felt it nonetheless. The currents shifted beneath the hull in ways that had no explanation in any sailor's almanac, running deep and strange, carrying with them the faint pressure of something enormous and old. Rides the Wake stood at the prow and said nothing, which was her way of saying she felt it too. Granit ran a thumb along the edge of a holy symbol and watched the horizon. The water here was not hostile. It was aware.

Shard C spread before them like a declaration. The Sea of Governance earned its name not from any single landmark but from the quality of the air above it — the sense that the very waves moved according to precedent, that the tides here had been argued over and ratified. Where Shard A had breathed with the lungs of something feral, and Shard B had exhaled a wrongness that clung to the spirit long after departure, this place carried weight of a different kind entirely. Human weight. The weight of ledgers and treaties and the violence done in the name of order. Lyvriele studied the approaching coastline with the patient attention she gave to an opponent she had not yet seen strike. Vargr Stormcaller closed his eyes and listened, and whatever the spirits of this water whispered to him, he kept to himself for now.

They made anchor as the light failed, the ship settling into the rhythm of a harbor that had received a thousand vessels before it and would receive a thousand more. The crossing was done. The work ahead was the slower, more treacherous kind — the kind that required full bellies, sharp steel, and a clear understanding of where one was going before one went. Charts would need to be sourced. Provisions secured. The party was whole and rested, their resources restored by the mercy of an uneventful passage, and that was no small thing in a world where uneventful passages were not guaranteed.

Whatever Shard C intended for them had not yet shown its face. The governance of these waters extended to something — some authority threatened or corrupted or contested — and the pattern of the Shards suggested it would be worse than what came before. Shard A had tested their bodies. Shard B had tested something closer to the soul. Here, in the domain of civilization's machinery, the test would be of a different order. Tonight, at least, the harbor was quiet, the water lapped against the hull with patient indifference, and the party slept while the strange deep currents moved beneath them toward whatever came next.
