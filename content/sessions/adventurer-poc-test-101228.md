The harbor smelled of salt and old rope and the particular kind of waiting that precedes something. The party arrived at the Shardsea of Governance with full bellies and empty ledgers, their ship cutting into port as the sun laid long shadows across the docks. After the raw elemental fury of Pyroskarn and the creeping wrongness of Tidecross, the Shardsea of Governance offered a different kind of unease — the kind that wore fine clothes and kept meticulous records. Nothing here would try to devour them outright. That, in its own way, made it more dangerous.

They moved through the port with the practiced ease of people who had learned that arriving somewhere and understanding somewhere are two entirely different things. Granit's heavy tread on the dock planking, Lyvriele's quiet assessment of every face that lingered too long, Rides the Wake reading the tide and the crowd with the same patient attention — Vargr, as ever, drawing it all in, filing it away, turning it over for meaning. The Shardsea of Governance was not a place that announced its threats. It implied them, buried in the language of ledgers and the architecture of who stood where.

What they needed was practical and immediate: rations for the next leg, charts of the local waters, some accounting of what this place was and who held authority over it. The Compass of Lost Tides, still warm from its vision of corrupted leylines, offered no new guidance — not yet. The darkness spreading across the world's arcane skeleton had not paused for their journey, and neither would whatever waited for them here among the merchants and the naval banners and the competing claims of civilization. The sea, as always, was patient. The party was learning to be as well.

Whatever came next in the Shardsea of Governance, it would not announce itself with volcanic fire or flooding shrines. It would arrive in a conversation, a contract, a name said just slightly too carefully by someone with a smile that didn't reach their eyes. The party had faced the world's fury and its corruption. Now they would face the thing that outlasts both: organized human interest, fortified behind procedure and precedent, ancient in the way that only compromise and accumulated power can be ancient. They had survived worse. They were not, perhaps, ready for something this civilized.
